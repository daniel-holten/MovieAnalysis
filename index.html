What Makes A "Good" Movie?¶

An analysis of movie data from 1916 to 2016 to determine the best way to make a successful movie.¶

By: Amy Zheng and Daniel Holten

Introduction:¶

The movie industry is a multi-billion dollar industry. According to a Deadline article, The U.S. film industry made over $43 billion in revenue in 2017 [1]. Movies bring in a serious amount of money every year but they can be extremely expensive to make, especially if you're shooting a big-budget star-studded, Oscar-worthy film. Producers need to know where to put their money to ensure that their films are a success. But what exactly determines a movie's success? How do we know what a "good" movie is? Well, there's many different ways you can look at how good a movie is. Everyone from film critics to filmmakers, cinephiles to casual movie viewers have their own different ways they determine what a good movie looks like. Some of those factors include: overall popularity, critic ratings, box-office earnings, theater run-time, awards, profit, and reviews.

In this tutorial, we will walk you through how we can use data and analytics to determine what really makes a good movie! Using data to analyze this is a great way to remove the biases that come with movie reviews. Everyone has varying opinions on movies. Just because a movie makes a huge amount of money and is liked by many people does not necessarily mean everyone views it as a "good" movie. Our goal is to determine how to make the best movie possible!

Part 3: Data Tidying¶

3.1 - Getting Rid of Bad/Unused Data¶

After you collect your data and get it into a useable format, you want to "clean it." Cleaning data means getting rid of things that you do not need or shouldn't have in your data. It can also mean changing the format of what's stored in your dataframe to a more readable or useable value. Getting rid of unusable datapoints or data we aren't going to use makes traversing through the data frame so much faster because it is smaller.

First, we dropped the columns we knew we weren't planning on using in our analysis (listed in labels). Since this is a rather large dataframe (4803 movies × 20 variables = 96,060 values), dropping 4 columns makes a large impact. Dropping the 4 columns we aren't using decreases our value count by around 19,000 and decreases the size of our dataframe by 1/5.

Next, we remove all rows with missing data in the budget and revenue columns. We cannot do analysis on a row that has missing data. This decreases our # of rows (movies) in our dataframe from 4083 to 3229, getting rid of around 850 unusable datapoints. This decreases our total value count by around 17,000.

Lastly, we discovered that this dataset has the wrong budget for a few movies. Some movies had an extremely low budget in this dataset ($1-$250). We checked the budget for all of these movies on different sites, and there they said the budget was actually much higher. Since the data was wrong we chose to remove them from the dataframe.

After removing unusable datapoints from our dataframe, we decreased our # of datapoints from 96,060 to 51,664. This step shrunk our dataframe by about 46%, decreasing the speed it takes to perform actions on our dataframe.

3.2 Convert Variables into Better Types¶

As of right now, each column that stores lists (genres, production_companies, production_countries, and spoken_languages) is storing each element as a JSON object. We cannot run analysis on objects (String, boolean, number, ...) so we need to convert them into a useable type. The following code traverses through the lists and removes all the unnecessary information from the JSON object. This leaves us with the actual values we need! For example, for production_countries, we just want a String list of country names. Doing this does exactly that.

Also, this converts the release dates into DateTime object so our dataframe recognizes that it's a date. This way we can organize movies by date and perform analysis on movies by release date.

#parses column and converts it to a list. Gets rid of the id and other random info. associated with the name
def parse_col(column, key):
    for index,i in zip(movies.index, movies[column].apply(json.loads)):
        list1=[]
        for j in range(len(i)):
            list1.append((i[j][key])) 
        movies.loc[index,column]=str(list1)
    
parse_col('genres', 'name')
parse_col('production_companies', 'name')
parse_col('production_countries', 'name')
parse_col('spoken_languages', 'name')

3.3 Add More to Data¶

We added three more columns to our dataframe to store additional information we thought would be necessary. One of them is profit. Profit is defined by the Net earnings a movie makes, which is revenue minus budget. This is important information because it helps us identify how successful a movie was in terms of box-office earnings. The higher the profit of a movie, the more successful that movie is seen. Also, we added a category that has each movie's release year. This is important so we can view trends between different years ranges, and notice trends over time. Finally, we added a category for profit margin. This is how well the movie performed at the box office based on it's budget. A profit margin above 1 is great because that means a movie made more money than it cost to make.

3.4 Combine Datasets¶

Analysis¶

The overall trend in the data shows that overtime, the average ratings of movies has been decreasing since about 1960. However, there was a large spike in the late 70s. Although this may look like the overall quality of movies is decreasing, that's not entirely true. People are still going to the movies, enjoying them, and rating them highly. So you might be asking yourself... then why does the average rating of a movie keep decreasing over time?

That's because the number of movies being rated is also increasing every year. As seen in the graph below, the number of ratings is extremely low for movies that were released from 1920 to 1980. Around 1990, the number of ratings started to skyrocket. With more and more ratings every year, its harder for the average rating to be as high as it used to be.

1927 is the year with the highest average rating of movies. However, there are only 657 ratings entered in TMDb's website for movies released that year. That's significantly less than the 200,000 ratings on the site for 2015 films.
